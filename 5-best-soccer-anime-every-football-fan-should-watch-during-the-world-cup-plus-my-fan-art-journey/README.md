I'm big on soccer, especially the FIFA World Cup. But this World Cup is different. For the first time, I'm not just watching matches. I'm rewatching some of my best soccer anime between games.

**How the 2026 World Cup Sent Me Back to Soccer Anime**

I just love the tournament atmosphere, from the flags to chants or the sheer "anything can happen" tension of a knockout match.

The real matches give me the stakes, but this time, those anime breaks between matches are giving me the drama the real matches can't always deliver. I'm loving the slow-motion close-ups, the internal monologues mid-shot, the rival who shows up out of nowhere with a backstory you didn't ask for but now desperately need.

So this World Cup, I fell back into a handful of soccer anime I genuinely love, for very different reasons. And somewhere around the third rewatch, I ended up somewhere I didn't expect: trying to make my own soccer anime fan art of these characters. More on that later. First, the anime.

**The 5 Best Soccer Anime I'd Recommend to Any Football Fan**

These aren't ranked. Each anime listed below scratches a different itch. If you only watch one, read the descriptions and pick whichever sounds like your kind of chaos.

**Blue Lock**

This one feels like a psychological thriller with a ball involved. It feels relatable right at the start when one team's equalizer attempt fails with the ball crashing off the crossbar, and within moments, the opponent team counters and scores. 2--0. That's it. That's football. Miss your chance, and the other side punishes you instantly.

The premise is gripping too: 300 of Japan's best young strikers get locked in a facility. They fight each other to become the nation's most egotistical striker.

What surprised me more was the bigger debate underneath it all: teamwork versus ego. Most sports anime use the sport as a backdrop for personality. Blue Lock makes the tension itself the whole point. That's the angle that sets it apart.

One scene that stuck with me is when Isagi chooses to challenge a tough player instead of a weak opponent. It reminds me of many Messi and Mbappé moments. Legends play their best football against tough teams in critical moments when it matters most.

The rest of the show lives up to that opening. Isagi keeps rewiring how he sees the game. And then there's Bachira. Feral, unpredictable, weirdly endearing. Everyone ends up loving him anyway.

*Isagi and Bachira quickly became two of my favorite characters in Blue Lock*

**Captain Tsubasa**

Watching Captain Tsubasa right after Blue Lock felt like football therapy. If Blue Lock is all about surviving the competition, Captain Tsubasa simply reminds you why you fell in love with football in the first place. Winning matters, but the love of the game comes first. And it's wild how much it still holds up, decades later.

That opening scene where baby Tsubasa survives a bus accident simply by holding onto his football immediately captures you and sets expectations for a larger-than-life soccer story.

What stands out watching it during a World Cup specifically is how international the story gets! Tsubasa's journey eventually pulls in players and rivalries from outside Japan. That's exactly what turns out to be true in real life given how many fans and pros have cited this series as a reason they fell in love with the game.

The visual style is classic and old-school, with bold lines, exaggerated kicks that seem to defy physics, but that's part of the charm.

*Captain Tsubasa reminds us why we love football*

**Ao Ashi**

Unlike Blue Lock and Captain Tsubasa, Ao Ashi is much more grounded. It portrays football as something you learn, not something you're magically born to dominate.

That scene where Ashito's coach draws circles in the field and asks him to control the ball into one of those circles using only one touch immediately signals what you can expect from the anime. And that's the whole show in miniature. It spends real time on the unglamorous stuff: positioning, decision-making, learning to see the whole field instead of just the ball at your feet.

It's the one I'd recommend to someone who actually watches a lot of real football and wants an anime that respects the tactical side of the sport instead of turning every match into a superpower showdown. The visual style matches that tone. It's grounded and controlled, with the occasional burst of intensity when Ashito finally clicks into form.

*Ao Ashi proves football is learned, not inherited*

**Giant Killing**

Most soccer anime put you on the pitch. Giant Killing puts you on the bench, next to the coach. It follows Tatsumi Takeshi, a coach with an unconventional approach, as he tries to drag a struggling team back into relevance.

The anime feels different from the beginning, especially as Tatsumi takes office as a manager. On his first day, Tatsumi chooses to shake up the team hierarchy, instead of accepting the established starters. That moment immediately signals exactly what kind of manager he's going to be. 

*Sometimes the biggest battles happen on the sidelines*

What makes it refreshing is the perspective shift. I felt like I was watching matches unfold as strategy and psychology as much as athleticism. It's slower and more deliberate than the others on this list, but if you've ever watched a real match and found yourself more interested in the manager's tactical adjustments than the players themselves, this one's for you.

**Days**

Days is the heart-on-its-sleeve pick. Tsukushi is about as far from a soccer prodigy as you can get. He's clumsy, untalented, and mostly just stubborn until he crosses paths with Jin Kazama, a genuine soccer prodigy who inspires him to give football a real shot. 

It's the most classically "underdog sports anime" entry on this list, and unapologetically so. The animation leans into raw effort over flashy technique, so that you can feel every sprint Tsukushi forces himself through. If Blue Lock makes you want to be the most talented player on the pitch, Days makes you want to just not give up. Different inspiration, but equally effective.

**Why Soccer Anime Makes Me Want to Create Fan Art**

Here's the thing about watching this much soccer anime during a tournament. At some point, a scene or character just sticks. And before you know it, you want to do more than just watch anime.

After spending a few evenings with these anime, I found myself wanting to recreate some of my favorite moments. Sometimes it was Isagi's intense stare before making a split-second decision. Other times it was Captain Tsubasa's powerful shots or the iconic team jerseys that caught my attention.

The more matches I watched, the more I caught myself thinking, "That would make an awesome piece of soccer anime fan art." I even thought "Wouldn't it be great if I could make wallpapers or a fun piece of anime soccer art of my favorite characters and post them on social media during the World Cup?"

*One of my original soccer anime character designs*

The curiosity eventually got the better of me, and I started experimenting with the idea. I didn't want to redraw the actual scene, but maybe just capture that same feeling with my own version of it. A character of my own, in my own composition, with that same dramatic stadium lighting and that same intensity.

The problem, for me, has always been the same one a lot of fans run into: I can't draw. Not even a little.

**How I Turned My World Cup Anime Binge Into Artwork**

Drawing skills aside, I was clear about one thing from the beginning. I didn't want to recreate the exact scene. I wanted to recreate the feeling. That's exactly what I had in mind when I opened the anime art generator [PixAI](https://eap.pixai.art/go/amit) for the first time.

*Opening PixAI was where the experiment really began*

My first instinct was to just generate Isagi. But pretty quickly I realized that wasn't actually what I wanted. Copying a character is a different exercise from making fan art. It's not creation, it's recreation. What I actually wanted was an original striker who felt like he belonged in that world. Someone who could exist in the Blue Lock universe without being a copy-paste of it.

So I started experimenting with prompts. The first few attempts were... fine. Technically a soccer player, technically anime-style, but completely lacking the mood I was after. Too flat, too generic and not yet the image rattling around in my head.

The real shift happened once I stopped trying to describe one anime at a time and started mixing influences instead. One prompt borrowed the dramatic stadium lighting from Blue Lock. Another leaned more toward Captain Tsubasa's bright, almost old-school color palette. 

That's when I learned the actual trick to prompting on PixAI: naming the anime barely helps. Simply typing "Blue Lock" wasn't enough to consistently get the look I had in mind. So instead of typing the show's name, I'd write something closer to: determined teenage striker, dramatic stadium lighting, blue jersey, rain, intense expression, dynamic action pose.

That kind of breakdown gave the model something to actually work with. And PixAI's Prompt Helper turned out to be useful here too, since I could describe what I wanted in a normal sentence and let it convert that into something more structured before generating.

*I found it more effective to describe the feeling I wanted than simply naming an anime*

A few rounds of tweaking later, I landed on an image that finally matched the mood in my head. It looked like my own striker in my own moment versus a redraw of a specific character.

Turns out writing prompts is much easier than learning to draw.

**Some Prompt Ideas That Worked Well**

I'm not going to pretend every generation was a hit. But here are a few prompt directions that consistently gave me the kind of results I was looking for, just in case you want to try something similar.

**Dynamic Striker Action**

This became my go-to whenever I wanted that high-energy Blue Lock feeling without recreating a specific character.

 *"Determined teenage soccer striker sprinting toward goal, blue jersey, dynamic action pose, flying grass, dramatic stadium lighting, cel-shaded anime style, intense expression"*

*Prompt and result for a dynamic striker action scene*

**Dramatic Goal Celebration**

I found myself returning to this prompt whenever I wanted to recreate those unforgettable post-goal moments that make soccer anime so exciting.

*"Young soccer player celebrating after scoring the winning goal, fists raised, roaring crowd, confetti, floodlights, vibrant anime illustration, emotional victory"*

*Prompt and result for a dramatic goal celebration scene*

**Stadium Lighting**

This one worked best when I wanted the lighting itself to carry the scene, the kind of moody, cinematic AI anime art that feels like the climax of an important match.

* "Original anime soccer player standing alone under bright stadium floodlights, rain falling, cinematic atmosphere, red jersey, dramatic shadows, detailed anime art"*

*Prompt and result for a dramatic stadium lighting scene*

**Cel-Shaded Anime Style**

For a cleaner anime look, focusing on the rendering style itself helped create a polished result without relying on a specific series.

*"Original teenage soccer player portrait, cel-shaded anime style, clean line art, bold colors, soft shading, modern sports anime aesthetic, confident expression"*

*Prompt and result for a clean cel-shaded anime portrait*

**Intense Facial Expression**

Close-up portraits worked especially well with this prompt, letting the expression alone carry the pressure and determination of a crucial World Cup-style moment. 

*"Close-up of a determined anime soccer striker, intense blue eyes, clenched jaw, beads of sweat, dramatic stadium lighting, emotional intensity, cinematic composition, detailed anime illustration, original character, looking directly at the viewer"*

*Using a facial-style LoRA alongside the prompt helped emphasize the character's expression*

**World Cup Atmosphere**

To recreate the excitement of the tournament itself, I leaned on this prompt to capture the feeling of walking into a packed stadium without copying any existing anime scene.

*"Original anime soccer player walking onto the pitch before kickoff, wearing a national team jersey, packed stadium, waving national flags, roaring crowd, colorful fan scarves, dramatic pre-match atmosphere, cinematic anime style, golden sunset lighting"*

*Prompt and result for capturing the excitement of a World Cup-style atmosphere*

For a couple of images, I also experimented with soccer-themed and anime-style LoRAs to make the characters feel a little more expressive. I treated them like seasoning rather than the whole recipe. The prompt still did most of the heavy lifting.

**Final Thoughts**

This World Cup turned into more than just watching matches for me. Between games, soccer anime gave me the drama, and somewhere along the way, it gave me the urge to create something of my own too.

I went from rewatching Isagi's intensity in Blue Lock to actually making my own version of that feeling, using [PixAI](https://eap.pixai.art/go/amit) to bring it to life. I'm still no artist. I just learned that I didn't need to be one.

If you are asking which soccer anime to watch first, start with whichever one matches your mood. You can watch Blue Lock for the chaos, Captain Tsubasa for classic football, Ao Ashi for the tactics, Giant Killing for the coach's perspective, and Days for the heart. And if a moment from any of them sticks with you the way mine did, it might be worth seeing what you can make out of it yourself.
